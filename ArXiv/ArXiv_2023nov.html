Modeling the Evolution from Massive Stars to Supernovae and Supernova Remnants (2311.05612)
Salvatore Orlando

Tags: SN ➞ SNR

Investigating the connection between young SNRs, parent SNe, and progenitor massive stars can be of paramount importance to delve into the physics of SN engines, and to investigate the final stages of massive star evolution and the elusive mechanisms governing their mass loss. This work report the recent advances of their team in modeling the path from massive stars to SNe and SNRs, focusing on the links between the observed physical and chemical properties of SNRs and their progenitor stars and SN explosions. The unraveling of this connection offers us the opportunity to probe the physics of core-collapse SN explosions and the final stages of evolution of massive stars.

Young SNRs (aged less than 5000 years) hold immense value ➞ crucial insights into the inner processes of the supernova (SN) engine + reveal details about nucleosynthetic yields and large-scale asymmetries arising from the early stages of the explosion;
Furthermore, these remnants also bear characteristics ➞ may reflect the nature of their progenitors and the interactions between the remnants and the surrounding CSM, shaped by the progenitor's mass-loss history.

JWST measurements of 13C, 18O and 17O in the atmosphere of super-Jupiter VHS 1256 b (2311.05349)
Siddharth Gandhi, Sam de Regt, Ignas Snellen, Yapeng Zhang, Benson Rugers, Niels van Leur, Quincy Bosschaart

Tags: Isotopic abundances Exoplanet atmospheric composition JWST

They constrain the carbon and oxygen isotopes 13C, 18O and 17O from CO in the atmosphere of the directly-imaged companion VHS 1256 b through retrievals of the ~ 4.1-5.3 μm NIRSpec G395H/F290LP observations.
They find the ratio of abundances are more precisely constrained than their absolute values, with 12C/13C=62 ± 2, in between previous measurements for companions (∼30) and isolated brown dwarfs (∼100). 16O/18O = 425+33-28, 16O/17O = 1010+120-100. These ratios are lower than the local ISM and Solar System, suggesting that abundances of the more minor isotopes are enhanced compared to the primary. This could be driven by isotope fractionation in protoplanetary disks, which can potentially alter the carbon and oxygen ratios through isotope selective photodissociation, gas/ice partitioning and isotopic exchange reactions.

07-Nov-2023 <Grain sizePolarization>

Exploring the dust grain size and polarization mechanism in the hot and massive Class 0 disk IRAS 16293-2422 B (2311.02521)
Joaquin Zamponi, María José Maureira, Hauyu Baobab Liu, Bo Zhao, Dominique Segura-Cox, Chia-Lin Ko, Paola Caselli

Tags: IRAS 16293 Continuum Continuum polarization Self-scattering Grain size

Multiwavelength dust continuum and polarization observations arising from self-scattering ➞ investigate grain sizes in young disks.
They studied the effects of changing the maximum grain sizes on the continuum and continuum polarization fractions from self-scattering, and compared a set of synthetic models with ALMA+VLA observations of IRAS 16293 B.
They concluded that significant grain growth could be present in the young Class 0 disk IRAS 16293 B, especially in the inner hot region (< 10 au, T > 300 K), where refractory organics evaporate. The polarization produced by self-scattering in their model is not high enough to explain the observations at 1.3 and 7 mm. Other possibilities, such as dichroic extinction or polarization reversal of elongated aligned grains, remain unexplored.

Self-scattering: the polarization of the dust thermal emission scattered by the dust itself. For grain sizes in the micro- and millimeter range, the polarized emission is produced to a few percent at millimeter wavelengths (Kataoka et al. 2017).
Self-scattering-origined polarized emission can be a useful independent method to constrain the grain sizes in disks (Lazarian & Hoang 2007a,b; Kataoka et al. 2015; Andersson et al. 2015; Tazaki et al. 2017).
The amount of polarization is affected by the anisotropy of the radiation field emitted from the self-scattered dust, which is determined by disk inclination and grain size (Yang et al. 2016). The link between grain size and maximum polarization makes this radiation a tool for estimating grain growth at different evolutionary stages.

In Class II protoplanetary disks, optical and near-IR observations typically trace scattered flux from small dust grains that scatter off the protostellar flux in the upper layers of the disk (Avenhaus et al. 2018; Garufi et al. 2018, 2019, 2020). At millimeter wavelengths, the observed dust scattered emission is mainly originated from the dust thermal emission (Kataoka et al. 2015; Yang et al. 2016).

IRAS 16293-2422 B is a well studied Class 0 protostar in ρ-Ophiuchi, and is one of the closest (141 pc, Dzib et al. 2018) and brightest protostars. The disk around the protostar is hot (Tb ≳ 400K) and massive, and likely subject to gravitational instabilities (Rodríguez et al. 2005, Dipierro et al. 2014, Zamponi et al. 2021). The source is very young (<104 yr, Andre et al. 1993) and its water snow line extends over a 20 au radius (Zamponi et al. 2021), making it an ideal laboratory to test the scenario that grain growth is boosted in dry conditions (proposed by recent laboratory experiments; Gundlach et al. 2018; Pillich et al. 2021; Li et al. 2021). The disk mass is estimated of 0.003 M☉ in solid material (Zamponi et al. 2021), which would be 33 times higher than the minimum mass solar nebula of 30 M⊕ (in solids; Weidenschilling 1977; Andrews 2020), implying that IRAS 16293 B contains enough mass to form super-Earth planetesimals.

06-Nov-2023 <PNe in bulgeRunaway stars>

A Catalogue of Planetary Nebulae Chemical Abundances in the Galactic Bulge (2311.01836)
Shuyu Tan, Quentin A. Parker, Albert A. Zijlstra, Bryan Rees

Tags: PNe Bulge Abundances

Using deep, long-slit FORS2 spectra from the 8.2 m ESO VLT ➞ well determine chemical abundances for PNe in the Galactic bulge.

Background about PNe

  • Bright (the most luminous phase in the evolved low- to intermediate-mass stars (~ 0.8 - 8 M☉); Typcal luminosity: 0.5 - 1.0 × 104 L☉)
  • Abundances ➞ interior nucleosynthesis + the interstellar abundances when the PN progenitor stars were born
    (PNe are the best abundance tracers of relatively old stellar populations in our own and external galaxies in the local group)

Bulge Metallicity

  • Previously available Bulge PNe abundance determinations have suggested a solar to slightly sub-solar metallicity (Ratag et al. 1992; Chiappini et al. 2009; Pottasch & Bernard-Salas 2015)
  • Spectroscopy of red giants and microlensed main sequence stars in the bulge show a rather wide abundance range (e.g., Johnson et al. 2014; Bensby et al. 2013)

Motivation
The observed differences between observed stellar and PNe abundances in the bulge remains problematic and impacts the question of the origin of the bulge, as either a pseudobulge, arising from scattered disk stars, or an old classical bulge. Hence, getting a better handle on a wider sample of Bulge PNe abundances could have significant implications.

Galactic runaway O and Be stars found using Gaia DR3 (2311.01827)
M. Carretero-Castrillo, M. Ribó, J. M. Paredes

Tags: O stars Be stars Runaway stars Gaia DR3

To discover and characterize the massive/early-type runaway stars in the GOSC (Galactic O-Star Catalog) and BeSS (Be Star Spectra database) catalogs using Gaia DR3. They found 106 (42 new) O runaway stars, 69 (47 new) Be runaway stars. The higher percentages and higher velocities found for O-type (25.4%) compared to Be-type (5.2%) runaways underline that the dynamical ejection scenario is more likely than the binary supernova scenario.

Be stars: a particular type of B stars that display Balmer emission lines and an excess of infrared emission because they present circumstellar envelopes in the form of decretion disks (Slettebak 1988; Rivinius et al. 2013).

Two possible scenarios to explain the existence of runaway stars: (1) the dynamical ejection scenario (DES), where a close three- or four-body interaction in the core of a dense cluster ejects a massive star (Poveda et al. 1967), and (2) the binary supernova scenario (BSS), where the mass loss in the supernova (SN) explosion of the most evolved star in a binary system induces either a runaway velocity to the remaining binary system, which now contains a compact object, or disrupts the binary system and induces runaway velocities to both the remaining massive OB star and the remaining compact object (Blaauw 1961).

Massive runaway stars could host compact companions (Bekenstein & Bowers 1974; Stone 1982; van Oijen 1989).
X-ray binaries (e.g., van den Heuvel et al. 2000); Gamma-ray binaries (Marcote et al. 2018).
Chini et al. (2012) obtained a binary fraction of 69% in a sample O runaway stars.

They define a runaway star as a star with a high peculiar 2D velocity (VTAN, WRSR) with respect to the mean Galactic rotation at a 3-sigma confidence level.
